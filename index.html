What is a Recession?

A recession is a significant decline in economic activity that affects households, businesses, and markets. While recessions are often associated with shrinking economic output, economists typically look at a wider range of indicators including spending, production, and employment.

Recessions are particularly damaging because they can become self-reinforcing. As consumers reduce spending, businesses face weaker demand and cut hiring and investment. Rising unemployment then further reduces household spending, exacerbating the downturn. Recessions also affect households through falling investment values, declining home prices, and tighter credit conditions. Businesses postpone expansion plans, cancel projects, or become more cautious about future spending. Governments experience lower tax revenues at the same time that demands for public assistance increase.

It is extraordinarily difficult to accurately predict a recession. Every day, millions of people, companies, and governments make decisions based on expectations about the future. While recessions may be triggered by specific shocks, their ultimate severity is determined by how the shocks spread throughout the broader markets. Economists rely on a wide range of indicators to assess recession risk, including those that consider the labor market, business activity, lending conditions, housing construction, consumer confidence, and financial markets. No single indicator is perfect, as some provide early warnings but occasionally produce false alarms (leading indicators), while others are highly reliable but only signal danger once a downturn has already begun (lagging indicators).

Recession Indicators

This Recession Indicators Dashboard contains some of the most commonly used figures for analyzing recession risks. They track different areas of the economy, but together they paint a more complete picture of the United States' economic health.

  • Yield Curve (10-Year minus 2-Year Treasury)Growth Expectations
    Think of U.S. Treasury bonds as loans to the government. Normally, lending money for 10 years pays more interest than lending for 2 years. When that flips — called an "inversion" — it means the bond market is betting that the economy will weaken and the Fed will be forced to cut rates. Every U.S. recession since 1970 was preceded by an inversion.
  • Corporate Bond Spread vs. TreasuryCredit Conditions
    Companies borrow money by issuing bonds. The extra interest they pay above what the government pays is called the "spread." When spreads widen sharply, it means investors are worried about companies defaulting, credit becomes more expensive, and businesses pull back on investment and hiring.
  • SLOOS — Senior Loan Officer SurveyBanking Sector
    Every quarter the Federal Reserve asks bank loan officers whether they are making it easier or harder to get a loan. When banks tighten standards, businesses and consumers can borrow less — less investment, fewer hires, and slower growth. A historically reliable recession warning sign.
  • ISM Manufacturing PMIBusiness Activity
    Each month the ISM surveys hundreds of purchasing managers: is business getting better or worse? Above 50 means expansion, below 50 means contraction. Because purchasing managers are ordering materials weeks in advance, this is a leading indicator — it often moves before the broader economy does.
  • Housing StartsHousing Market
    This counts how many new homes began construction in a given month. Housing is one of the most interest-rate-sensitive parts of the economy. A sharp sustained drop is a reliable early warning sign because it signals that consumers are pulling back on big purchases and construction employment is about to fall.
  • Sahm RuleLabor Market
    Created by economist Claudia Sahm, this rule says: if the 3-month average unemployment rate rises by 0.5 percentage points or more from its lowest point in the past year, a recession has almost certainly begun. It is a real-time detector — not a predictor — and has triggered in every U.S. recession since 1950.
  • Weekly Jobless ClaimsLabor Market
    Every week, the government counts how many people filed for unemployment benefits for the first time — the most current labor market data available, just a week old. When this number starts climbing steadily, it means employers are cutting workers. Low readings (under 250K) indicate a healthy job market.
  • UMich Consumer SentimentConsumer Confidence
    The University of Michigan surveys households each month asking how they feel about their personal finances and the economy. Since consumer spending accounts for about 70% of U.S. GDP, when people feel bad about the future they tend to spend less — and that reduced spending can itself cause a recession.
  • Real Personal Income Excluding TransfersConsumer Confidence
    This tracks how much money people are taking home from work, adjusted for inflation, and excluding government benefits. Stripping out transfers gives a cleaner read on whether earned purchasing power is genuinely growing. Sustained declines are one of the primary criteria the NBER uses to officially date recessions.
  • Nondefense Capital Goods Orders Excluding AircraftBusiness Investment
    This measures new orders placed with U.S. manufacturers for long-lasting equipment — things like industrial machinery, computers, and communications gear — excluding volatile defense contracts and aircraft orders. One of the best real-time proxies for business investment intentions. When companies are confident about the future, they order more equipment; when they are worried, they pull back.
  • S&P 500Stock Market
    The S&P 500 tracks the 500 largest U.S. companies. Stock prices are forward-looking — investors are constantly betting on what profits will look like months from now. A sustained drop of 20% or more often signals that professional investors expect earnings and the economy to deteriorate significantly.

    History of Recessions

    The United States has experienced 23 recessions since 1900, driven by a wide variety of causes including financial panics, speculative excess, supply shocks, monetary policy errors, and external geopolitical events. While no two recessions are identical, they share a common thread: vulnerabilities that had been building beneath the surface, ignited by a triggering shock that exposed them. Below are three of the most consequential downturns in modern history.

    1929 – 1939
    The Great Depression
    Peak: Aug 1929 · Trough: Mar 1933 · Duration: 43 months
    Underlying Vulnerabilities
    The 1920s appeared prosperous on the surface, but concealed profound structural weaknesses. Agricultural prices had been depressed throughout the decade, leaving rural banks fragile. Industrial output had outpaced consumer purchasing power, with credit-financed demand masking the gap. Equity markets were driven to speculative excess by buying on thin margins — investors could purchase stocks with as little as 10% down. The gold standard constrained monetary flexibility, and the global banking system lacked any meaningful deposit insurance or lender-of-last-resort infrastructure.
    The Spark
    The stock market crash of October 1929 — Black Thursday and Black Tuesday — wiped out billions in paper wealth and triggered a cascade of margin calls. But the crash alone did not cause the Depression; the policy response did. The Federal Reserve tightened monetary policy to defend gold reserves, raising interest rates into a contracting economy. A wave of bank failures swept the country between 1930 and 1933, destroying savings and collapsing credit supply. The Smoot-Hawley Tariff Act of 1930 triggered retaliatory trade barriers globally, cutting world trade by roughly 65%.
    Second and Third Order Effects
    Unemployment reached 25%. GDP fell nearly 30% from peak to trough. A deflationary spiral took hold — falling prices made debt more burdensome, further suppressing demand. Internationally, the Depression destabilized democracies across Europe, contributing to the conditions that enabled the rise of authoritarian governments. The Dust Bowl simultaneously devastated agricultural regions, displacing hundreds of thousands of families.
    Policy Response and Lessons
    FDR's New Deal (1933–1939) deployed large-scale fiscal stimulus, created the FDIC to restore confidence in banks, established Social Security, and took the U.S. off the gold standard domestically. WWII spending ultimately ended the Depression. The lessons were foundational: deposit insurance prevents bank runs, automatic stabilizers smooth cycles, monetary policy must not tighten into a contraction, and the gold standard imposes catastrophic constraints in a crisis.
    1965 – 1982
    The Great Stagflation
    Two recessions: Nov 1973–Mar 1975, Jul 1981–Nov 1982
    Underlying Vulnerabilities
    Post-WWII prosperity had embedded expectations of perpetual growth and low inflation. The Federal Reserve, under political pressure, allowed money supply growth to run ahead of productive capacity throughout the late 1960s to finance both the Vietnam War and Great Society spending — the "guns and butter" era. This overheated an already tight labor market, embedding inflation into wage contracts. The U.S. abandonment of the Bretton Woods gold standard in 1971 removed the last external anchor on monetary policy.
    The Spark
    The Arab oil embargo of October 1973 quadrupled energy prices overnight, simultaneously raising costs for every sector of the economy while reducing consumer purchasing power. This was a supply shock, not a demand shock — the conventional tools of monetary stimulus would only worsen inflation without restoring output. A second oil shock followed the Iranian Revolution in 1979. The economy faced simultaneous high inflation and high unemployment — "stagflation" — a combination the prevailing Keynesian framework had not anticipated.
    Second and Third Order Effects
    Inflation peaked above 14% in 1980. Core PCE exceeded 10%. The "misery index" (inflation plus unemployment) hit 22%. Middle-class purchasing power eroded sharply. Fixed-income retirees were devastated. Business investment collapsed as real interest rates became highly uncertain. Public confidence in government economic management fell to historic lows. The episode permanently altered macroeconomic thinking about the relationship between output and inflation.
    Policy Response and Lessons
    Fed Chairman Paul Volcker deliberately induced a severe recession in 1981–82 by raising the federal funds rate to 20%, crushing inflation at the cost of 10.8% peak unemployment. It worked: inflation fell from 14% to 3% by 1983, and three decades of relative price stability followed. The lesson was painful: once inflation expectations become unanchored, the cure is more costly than the disease. Central bank independence and credible inflation targeting became cornerstones of modern monetary policy.
    2007 – 2009
    The Great Recession
    Peak: Dec 2007 · Trough: Jun 2009 · Duration: 18 months
    Underlying Vulnerabilities
    A decade of financial deregulation, low interest rates, and financial innovation had built extraordinary leverage into the global financial system. Mortgage originators had abandoned underwriting standards, issuing loans to borrowers with no income documentation or down payments — "NINJA" loans. These were packaged into complex securities (CDOs, MBS) and sold to global investors, with credit rating agencies assigning them investment-grade ratings. Insurance products (credit default swaps) written by undercapitalized counterparties created a web of interconnected exposures that nobody fully understood.
    The Spark
    U.S. home prices, which had doubled between 1997 and 2006, began declining in 2006. As adjustable-rate mortgages reset higher, default rates climbed. By 2007 major subprime lenders were failing. In September 2008, the decision to let Lehman Brothers — a firm with $600 billion in assets — fail without a rescue triggered a global financial panic. Credit markets froze. Money market funds "broke the buck." Interbank lending ceased. The system that had funded the global economy for decades locked up within days.
    Second and Third Order Effects
    U.S. unemployment rose from 4.7% to 10%. Nearly 9 million jobs were lost. Home values fell 30% nationally; trillions in household wealth evaporated. The global financial system contracted simultaneously, turning a U.S. financial crisis into a global recession. Trade finance collapsed, hitting export-dependent economies worldwide. The Eurozone sovereign debt crisis (2010–2012) was a direct aftershock. Millions of Americans permanently exited the workforce.
    Policy Response and Lessons
    The Fed cut rates to zero and deployed unconventional tools — quantitative easing, emergency lending facilities — on an unprecedented scale. TARP provided $700B in bank recapitalization. The American Recovery and Reinvestment Act added $787B in fiscal stimulus. Dodd-Frank (2010) overhauled financial regulation, creating the CFPB and imposing new capital requirements on systemically important banks. The lessons: leverage limits matter, financial innovation can hide systemic risk, and the failure of a systemically critical institution cannot be allowed to cascade uncontrolled.
