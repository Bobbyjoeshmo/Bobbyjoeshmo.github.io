What is a Recession and Why Does it Matter?

A recession is a widespread and prolonged downturn in economic activity. In simple terms, it is when the economy shrinks rather than grows. The most common rule of thumb is two consecutive quarters of negative GDP growth, though the official arbiter in the U.S. — the National Bureau of Economic Research (NBER) — looks at a broader set of factors including employment, spending, and production.

During a recession, businesses earn less and cut costs, which usually means layoffs. Unemployment rises, consumers spend less, and companies invest less. This creates a feedback loop: less spending leads to lower business revenue, which leads to more job cuts, which leads to even less spending. Stock markets typically fall sharply, housing markets cool, and credit becomes harder to get.

The pain is not spread evenly. Lower-income households feel recessions most acutely — they are more likely to lose jobs, have less savings to cushion the blow, and spend a greater share of income on necessities whose prices tend to stay elevated even as wages fall.

Why are Recessions so Hard to Predict?

The economy is extraordinarily complex — millions of people, companies, and governments making decisions simultaneously based on expectations about the future. Economic indicators are published with a delay and can send conflicting signals. Even the world's best economists have repeatedly failed to predict recessions in advance. Most recessions are only officially confirmed after they have already begun. This dashboard tracks a basket of the most reliable leading and coincident indicators to give a clearer overall picture — no single number tells the whole story.

The Indicators

Below are 12 commonly used recession indicators, each measuring a different corner of the economy. Together they paint a more complete picture of economic health than any single metric could on its own. Click on any indicator card on the data tabs to see detailed thresholds and historical trends.

  • Yield Curve (10-Year minus 2-Year Treasury)Growth Expectations
    Think of U.S. Treasury bonds as loans to the government. Normally, lending money for 10 years pays more interest than lending for 2 years. When that flips — called an "inversion" — it means the bond market is betting that the economy will weaken and the Fed will be forced to cut rates. Every U.S. recession since 1970 was preceded by an inversion.
  • Core PCE InflationInflation
    This measures how fast prices are rising for everyday goods and services, excluding food and energy. It is the Federal Reserve's preferred inflation gauge. When inflation is too high, the Fed raises interest rates to cool things down — but high rates also slow growth and can tip the economy into recession.
  • Corporate Bond Spread vs. TreasuryCredit Conditions
    Companies borrow money by issuing bonds. The extra interest they pay above what the government pays is called the "spread." When spreads widen sharply, it means investors are worried about companies defaulting, credit gets more expensive, and businesses pull back on investment and hiring.
  • SLOOS — Senior Loan Officer SurveyBanking Sector
    Every quarter the Federal Reserve asks bank loan officers whether they are making it easier or harder to get a loan. When banks tighten standards, businesses and consumers can borrow less — less investment, fewer hires, and slower growth. A historically reliable recession warning sign.
  • ISM Manufacturing PMIBusiness Activity
    Each month the ISM surveys hundreds of purchasing managers: is business getting better or worse? Above 50 means expansion, below 50 means contraction. Because purchasing managers are ordering materials weeks in advance, this is a leading indicator — it often moves before the broader economy does.
  • Housing StartsHousing Market
    This counts how many new homes began construction in a given month. Housing is one of the most interest-rate-sensitive parts of the economy. A sharp sustained drop is a reliable early warning sign because it signals that consumers are pulling back on big purchases and construction employment is about to fall.
  • Sahm RuleLabor Market
    Created by economist Claudia Sahm, this rule says: if the 3-month average unemployment rate rises by 0.5 percentage points or more from its lowest point in the past year, a recession has almost certainly begun. It is a real-time detector — not a predictor — and has triggered in every U.S. recession since 1950.
  • Weekly Jobless ClaimsLabor Market
    Every week, the government counts how many people filed for unemployment benefits for the first time — the most current labor market data available, just a week old. When this number starts climbing steadily, it means employers are cutting workers. Low readings (under 250K) indicate a healthy job market.
  • UMich Consumer SentimentConsumer Confidence
    The University of Michigan surveys households each month asking how they feel about their personal finances and the economy. Since consumer spending accounts for about 70% of U.S. GDP, when people feel bad about the future they tend to spend less — and that reduced spending can itself cause a recession.
  • Real Personal Income Excluding TransfersConsumer Confidence
    This tracks how much money people are taking home from work, adjusted for inflation, and excluding government benefits. Stripping out transfers gives a cleaner read on whether earned purchasing power is genuinely growing. Sustained declines are one of the primary criteria the NBER uses to officially date recessions.
  • S&P 500Stock Market
    The S&P 500 tracks the 500 largest U.S. companies. Stock prices are forward-looking — investors are constantly betting on what profits will look like months from now. A sustained drop of 20% or more (a "bear market") often signals that professional investors expect earnings and the economy to deteriorate significantly.
  • Russell 2000Stock Market
    The Russell 2000 tracks 2,000 smaller U.S. companies. Small businesses are more exposed to domestic conditions and rely more on bank loans to operate. When the Russell 2000 significantly underperforms the S&P 500, it often signals that credit is tightening or domestic growth is weakening — risks that hit smaller companies first.
