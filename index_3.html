What is a Recession — and Why Does it Matter?

A recession is a significant, widespread, and prolonged downturn in economic activity. In simple terms, it's when the economy shrinks rather than grows. The most common rule of thumb is two consecutive quarters of negative GDP growth, though the official arbiter in the U.S. — the National Bureau of Economic Research (NBER) — looks at a broader set of factors including employment, income, spending, and industrial production.

During a recession, businesses earn less and cut costs, which usually means layoffs. Unemployment rises, consumers spend less, and companies invest less. This creates a feedback loop: less spending leads to lower business revenue, which leads to more job cuts, which leads to even less spending. Stock markets typically fall sharply, housing markets cool, and credit becomes harder to get.

The pain isn't spread evenly. Lower-income households feel recessions most acutely — they're more likely to lose jobs, have less savings to cushion the blow, and spend a greater share of income on necessities whose prices tend to stay high even as wages fall.

Why are recessions so hard to predict?
The economy is extraordinarily complex — millions of people, companies, and governments making decisions simultaneously based on expectations about the future. Economic indicators are published with a delay (sometimes weeks or months after the fact), revised after the fact, and can send conflicting signals. Even the world's best economists and central banks have repeatedly failed to predict recessions in advance. Most recessions are only officially confirmed after they've already begun. This dashboard tracks a basket of the most reliable leading and coincident indicators to give a clearer overall picture — no single number tells the whole story.
The indicators — explained in plain English
  • Yield Curve (10-Year minus 2-Year Treasury) Growth Expectations
    Think of U.S. Treasury bonds as loans to the government. Normally, lending money for 10 years pays more interest than lending for 2 years — you're taking on more risk over a longer time. When that flips (called an "inversion") and short-term rates are higher than long-term rates, it means the bond market is betting that the economy will weaken and the Federal Reserve will be forced to cut rates in the future. Every U.S. recession since 1970 was preceded by an inversion.
  • Core PCE Inflation Inflation
    This measures how fast prices are rising for everyday goods and services, excluding food and energy (which are volatile). It's the Federal Reserve's preferred inflation gauge. When inflation is too high, the Fed raises interest rates to cool things down — but high rates also slow growth and can tip the economy into recession. Core PCE above ~3% ties the Fed's hands, preventing it from cutting rates to soften a slowdown.
  • Corporate Bond Spread vs. Treasury Credit Conditions
    Companies borrow money by issuing bonds. The extra interest they pay above what the government pays is called the "spread" — it reflects how risky investors think it is to lend to businesses. When spreads widen sharply, it means investors are worried about companies defaulting, credit gets more expensive, and businesses pull back on investment and hiring. It's a real-time fear gauge for the corporate sector.
  • SLOOS — Senior Loan Officer Survey Banking Sector
    Every quarter the Federal Reserve asks bank loan officers whether they're making it easier or harder to get a loan. When banks tighten standards — requiring better credit scores, more collateral, or simply saying "no" more often — businesses and consumers can borrow less. Less borrowing means less investment, fewer hires, and slower growth. Tighter lending has historically been one of the clearest recession warning signs.
  • ISM Manufacturing PMI Business Activity
    Each month, the Institute for Supply Management surveys hundreds of purchasing managers at manufacturing companies and asks: is business getting better or worse? The result is a single number — above 50 means expansion, below 50 means contraction. Because purchasing managers are ordering materials and planning production weeks in advance, this is a leading indicator — it often moves before the broader economy does.
  • Housing Starts Housing Market
    This counts how many new homes began construction in a given month. Housing is one of the most interest-rate-sensitive parts of the economy — when rates rise, mortgages get more expensive, fewer people can buy homes, and builders stop building. A sharp sustained drop in housing starts is a reliable early warning sign because it signals both that consumers are pulling back on big purchases and that construction employment is about to fall.
  • Sahm Rule Labor Market
    Created by economist Claudia Sahm, this rule says: if the 3-month average unemployment rate rises by 0.5 percentage points or more from its lowest point in the past year, a recession has almost certainly already begun. It's not a predictor — it's a real-time detector. The logic is simple: once unemployment starts rising meaningfully, layoffs tend to cascade. It has triggered in every U.S. recession since 1950.
  • Weekly Jobless Claims Labor Market
    Every week, the government counts how many people filed for unemployment benefits for the first time. It's the most current labor market data available — just a week old. When this number starts climbing steadily, it means employers are cutting workers. Low readings (under 250K) indicate a healthy job market; sustained readings above 300K–350K are associated with recessions.
  • UMich Consumer Sentiment Consumer Confidence
    The University of Michigan surveys around 500 households each month and asks how they feel about their personal finances and the economy. Since consumer spending accounts for about 70% of U.S. GDP, when people feel bad about the future, they tend to spend less — and that reduced spending can itself cause a recession. It's both a reflection of conditions and a driver of them.
  • Real Personal Income Excluding Transfers Consumer Confidence
    This tracks how much money people are actually taking home from work (wages and salaries), adjusted for inflation, and excluding government benefits like Social Security. Stripping out transfers gives a cleaner read on whether people's earned purchasing power is genuinely growing. Sustained declines in this measure are one of the primary criteria the NBER uses to officially date recessions.
  • S&P 500 Stock Market
    The S&P 500 tracks the 500 largest U.S. companies. Stock prices are forward-looking — investors are constantly betting on what profits will look like months or years from now. A sustained drop of 20% or more (a "bear market") often signals that professional investors expect earnings and the economy to deteriorate significantly. However, stocks can also fall without a recession following, so this indicator is best read alongside the others.
  • Russell 2000 Stock Market
    The Russell 2000 tracks 2,000 smaller U.S. companies. Small businesses are more exposed to domestic economic conditions and rely more heavily on bank loans to operate. When the Russell 2000 significantly underperforms the S&P 500, it often signals that credit is tightening or domestic growth is weakening — risks that tend to hit smaller companies before large multinationals.
